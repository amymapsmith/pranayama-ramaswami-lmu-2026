Pranayama

Notes from the four-day Vinyasa Krama seminar led by Srivatsa Ramaswami — a direct student of Krishnamacharya, and my first time learning from him in person. These are personal study notes I'm sharing as a resource for anyone curious about pranayama. For those who want to go deeper, Sri Ramaswami has an excellent YouTube channel and email newsletter. The seminar covered far more than pranayama, but it was the area I knew least and am most focused on building into my practice.

What is Pranayama?

Two Sanskrit roots: prana (the life force, or vayu, in the body) and ayama (to extend or bring under control). Together: voluntary regulation of the life force through breath.

"Prana refers to the vayu (force) in the body. Aayama is to completely control it — to bring it under voluntary control." — Ramaswami, Sagarbha Pranayama

Breathing is normally shallow and involuntary. Pranayama changes that. Bring breath under deliberate control, and other involuntary functions of body and mind follow. Ramaswami called it the most essential aspect of living. The root aya means to make longer; yama means to span.

"The yogis have found that by exercising control on Prana, one can control the mind and thereafter transcend it." — Ramaswami, Sagarbha Pranayama
The Prashna Upanishad says that prana is sharira dharaka — the holder of the body. The moment prana leaves the body, the body collapses. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 3)

Prerequisites

Patanjali's definition (YS 2.49) opens with a condition: tasmin sati, "with that established." The "that" is asana.

"Remaining in a posture (which is comfortable and steady), controlling the inward and outward movement of breath is pranayama." — Patanjali, Yoga Sutras 2.49, cited in Sagarbha Pranayama
My teacher would never allow me to leave the class without doing pranayama practice. Every day — we have to include pranayama every day. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 2)

Asana isn't just structural preparation. Vinyasa krama practice conditions the respiratory apparatus and addresses blood and pranic circulation. By the time you sit for pranayama, the body is ready to actually be still.

Practical note from the seminar: Ramaswami recommended taking a small sip of water and emptying the bladder before pranayama practice. Practice should ideally include equal parts asana, pranayama, and dharana. This ratio can shift with life stage: younger practitioners benefit from more asana; elderly practitioners benefit from more dharana.

The Four Components

Pranayama is not simply breathing. It has four distinct phases.

1. Inhalation
Puraka

Complete drawing in of air through the nostrils.

2. Internal Retention
Antahkumbhaka

Holding the breath in after inhalation.

3. Exhalation
Rechaka

Complete expelling of air from the lungs.

4. External Retention
Bahyakumbhaka

Holding the breath out after complete exhalation. The breath is held using bandhas (locks).

"Pranayama is the act of preventing both the activities of inhalation and exhalation. Even this is of two kinds. One is holding the breath after complete inhalation and the other is to hold the 'breath out' as it were, after complete exhalation." — Ramaswami, Sagarbha Pranayama

The retention (kumbhaka) is what distinguishes pranayama from simple breathing exercises.

Breath Ratios

From the Sagarbha Pranayama essay: Patanjali identifies three qualities of pranayama — desha (place: where in the body the breath is felt), kaala (time: duration of each phase), and samkhya (number: repetitions). All three matter.

The most widely used classical ratio is 1:4:2: inhale for one unit, retain for four, exhale for two.

Phase Ratio Example (seconds)
Puraka: Inhale 1 5 sec
Antahkumbhaka: Retain 4 20 sec
Rechaka: Exhale 2 10 sec

Alternative duration across all phases is also valid. What matters is consistency within a sitting and slow development over time.

Key principle: Ratios must be developed slowly. Never force breath retention. The capacity to hold comfortably for the prescribed duration of mantra (64 syllables, ~20 seconds) is built through patient, gradual practice.

Bandhas in Pranayama

Bandhas (locks) are internal muscular contractions applied during kumbhaka.

Ramaswami mentioned bandhas primarily at the end of exhalation (before or during bahyakumbhaka). Patanjali refers to this indirectly via desha paridrishti (the "witnessed placement" of held breath in specific regions of the body).

From the Sagarbha Pranayama essay: classical texts describe the held breath, directed by bandhas, as forcing "circulation from the roots of hair and the end of nails."

Agarbha and Sagarbha

Two types, based on whether mantra and dhyana are incorporated:

Without mantra
Agarbha (Amantraka)

Breath regulation practiced without a mantra or contemplative object. The breath itself is regulated, but there is no internal recitation.

With mantra
Sagarbha (Samantraka)

Breath regulation practiced with mantra and dhyana woven into each phase.

"Agarbha is to practice breath control without dhyana and Sagarbha is such a practice with an object for contemplation." — Narada, cited in Ramaswami's Sagarbha Pranayama

The Techniques

Kapalabhati, ujjayi, and bastrika each address a different physiological condition. They're not interchangeable.

The respiratory tract is not one unit — there are different parts to it, different aspects. Just as the body has different parts and you must exercise them with different asanas, you must exercise the different parts of the respiratory system. So you have different pranayamas. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 1)
Ujjayi ujjāyī

A slow, controlled breath with gentle constriction at the throat, producing a soft hissing sound. Ramaswami described it as "very unique to yoga" and noted its similarity to the breathing pattern in bronchial asthma: it targets the tracheal and bronchial area specifically.

Different variations exist: inhale with ujjayi and exhale through one nostril (Anuloma Ujjayi); inhale through one nostril and exhale with ujjayi (Pratiloma Ujjayi); or both phases through the throat.

  • Throat slightly contracted, producing a mild rubbing sensation
  • Soft hissing sound on inhale and/or exhale
  • Works on the tracheal and bronchial area
  • Forms the base breath for more complex pranayama variations
Kapalabhati kapālabhāti

Forceful, rapid exhalations driven by sharp contractions of the diaphragm, with passive inhalations between them.

Amy's notes: kapalabhati corrects an overactive sympathetic nervous system, specifically the muscles of the diaphragm that trigger overly reactive diaphragmatic sweeping. It calms tamas (heaviness, sleepiness) and is one of the primary antidotes to the first enemy of meditation.

  • Forceful exhalation, passive inhalation
  • Rhythmic abdominal pumping
  • Clears respiratory passages
  • Addresses tamas: the heaviness or dullness that prevents meditation
Bastrika bhastrīkā

"Bellows breath." Rapid, forceful inhalations and exhalations, both active, unlike kapalabhati which has a passive inhale. Named for the blacksmith's bellows.

Amy's notes: bastrika addresses overactive coughing and lung irritation. Where kapalabhati calms the diaphragm, bastrika works on the lungs and upper airways more forcefully.

  • Both inhale and exhale are vigorous
  • Generates heat and stimulates the system
  • Used for lung clearing and addressing respiratory congestion
Nadi Shodhana nāḍī śodhana

Alternate nostril breathing. Inhalation through one nostril, retention, exhalation through the other, then the pattern reverses. The right nostril is controlled with the right thumb; the left with the ring and little fingers. The index and middle fingers are never used.

The classical method: inhale through the left nostril (ida), retain with mantra, exhale through the right nostril (pingala); then reverse.

  • Ideal vehicle for sagarbha practice with mantra during kumbhaka
  • Traditional: left nostril inhale → kumbhaka → right nostril exhale, alternate

Pranayama and the Nervous System

Breath is the only major vital function that is both voluntary and involuntary. Ramaswami drew on yogic teaching and neurophysiology to explain why that matters: it's the yogi's direct entry point into the autonomic nervous system.

"Respiratory function can be more easily influenced than any other vital function and the yogi uses it as a first step in his control of the nervous system. When cortical higher brain control is achieved over one basic function, it is possible to achieve control over other basic functions." — Ramaswami, Sagarbha Pranayama

From the Sagarbha Pranayama essay: Patanjali calls this chitta parinama (alteration of brain cells). Consistent practice "quite likely leads to an enlargement of the scope of the reticular function and of the cortex." Like a farmer diverting water through a field, the yogi only has to direct neurological energies along certain paths.

Breath and manas are good friends. When the mind is disturbed, the breath is disturbed. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 2)

Gunas, Mind, and Pranayama

Ramaswami mapped mental conditions to the three gunas and named which pranayamas address each:

Guna Quality How it Manifests
Sattva Clarity, lightness Clear, present mind; the desired state for meditation
Rajas Restlessness, agitation Active, flighty mind; annoyances and tensions build up
Tamas Heaviness, dullness Sleepy mind; waste products and lethargy accumulate
Yama and niyama help you reduce unnecessary mental disturbances. Asana helps you overcome unnecessary bodily vrittis. Pranayama removes the chatter in your mind. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 4)

The two enemies of meditation: lethargy and an overactive mind. Excess tamas, excess rajas. Pranayama comes before meditation specifically to address both and bring the mind to a sattvic state.

"Tatah kshiyate prakaasa aavaranam — With pranayama, the clouding of the mind reduces and the intellect shines in its true splendour." — Patanjali, Yoga Sutras 2.52, cited by Ramaswami
If you practice pranayama regularly for a few days, there is a complete change in the way the mind functions. The chatter starts coming down. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 4)

Pranayama's Place in Ashtanga

Pranayama is the fourth limb of Patanjali's eight-limbed path. Each limb prepares the next:

Yama
→
Niyama
→
Asana
→
Pranayama
→
Pratyahara
→
Dharana
→
Dhyana
→
Samadhi
Just as vinyasas are necessary preparation for asanas, pranayama and asana are necessary for meditation — because they reduce excess mental production. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 1)

Asana gets the body still. Pranayama steadies the mind. As breath settles, sensory activity naturally decreases: pratyahara.

Asana → pranayama → pratyahara → dharana. Once you start meditation, you are the teacher. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 1)

Dharanasu cha yogyata manasah: from pranayama, the mind becomes fit for dharana. That's the gateway to the inner practice.

Ramaswami was clear: equal parts asana, pranayama, and dharana. A session divides into thirds. This ratio can shift with life stage: younger practitioners benefit from more asana; elderly practitioners benefit from more dharana. Most modern practitioners spend nearly all their time on asana — a significant departure from the classical model.

Practice Guidelines

Posture

Sit in a stable, comfortable seated position. Upright spine.

Preparation

Small sip of water, empty bladder, complete asana practice first.

Consistency

As you get older, the lung capacity comes down — the alveoli become less elastic. That is why, on a daily basis, you have to stretch them, so that the elasticity is maintained and you can have good breathing capacity for a longer period of time. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 2)
Just like you enjoy asana practice, if you regularly practice pranayama, you will also enjoy it. It takes a little while, and later on, the mind slowly settles down and begins to enjoy the peace that pranayama produces. — Ramaswami, seminar notes (Day 2)

Developing Kumbhaka Gradually

Never force retention. Start with whatever duration feels effortless. Extend incrementally over months.
